HL7 or FHIR —
which one fits your situation?

Not sure whether you need HL7 or FHIR? You're not alone — this question confuses clinicians, product managers, and founders every day. Answer six plain-English questions and get a reasoned recommendation with supporting evidence. No jargon required.

Question 1 of 6
Question 1 of 6 — What are you trying to do?
Pick the option that best describes the main job this connection needs to do.
Don't worry about getting this perfect — choose the closest fit. You can always start over.
Question 2 of 6 — The systems involved
How would you describe the technology you're connecting?
Think about the system where the data currently lives and where it needs to go. If in doubt, "older" means it was set up more than 10 years ago and hasn't been significantly updated.
Question 3 of 6 — Direction of data flow
How should data move between the systems?
This is one of the most important questions. Think about whether data is pushed out automatically or pulled in on request.
Question 4 of 6 — Where you're based
Which country or region does this apply to?
Different countries have different rules about which standards must be used. This shapes the recommendation — especially for regulatory projects.
Question 5 of 6 — What's already in place
What integration technology, if any, does your organisation already use?
Existing infrastructure matters — it affects cost, timeline, and risk. If you're not sure, ask your IT team what software they use to connect hospital systems.
Question 6 of 6 — Who uses the data
Who will actually see or act on the data this connection produces?
The end user of the data is one of the strongest signals for which standard to choose. Think about who benefits most from the connection.
Signal Strength — how strongly your answers point to each standard
Each answer adds evidence for one standard or the other. A large gap = high confidence. A close gap = a hybrid approach may be right.
How to read the confidence score
45–60%
Mixed signals. Both standards have real merit here. Seek specialist advice before deciding.
61–74%
Moderate confidence. One standard is the better fit, but there are real trade-offs to manage.
75–95%
High confidence. Answers consistently point one way. Proceed — but verify your specific vendor's support.
The score is capped at 95% — never 100%. The remaining 5% accounts for your specific vendor capabilities, local context, and regulations that may have changed. Real implementation decisions always need human expert review.

        The scoring logic in this tool is grounded in the following published sources. The tool weighs answers against documented decision criteria from HL7 International, national health authorities, and peer-reviewed research.
        • Firely — State of FHIR 2025 Survey Primary source
          73% of countries with electronic health data regulations now mandate or advise FHIR (up from 65% in 2024). Used to weight regulatory context globally. fire.ly →
        • HL7 International — FHIR R4 Specification Primary source
          FHIR is designed as a RESTful, query-based standard; HL7 v2 is designed for event-driven, push-based messaging. Used to weight Q1 (purpose) and Q3 (data flow). hl7.org →
        • Rhapsody Health — FHIR vs HL7 (Updated 2025)
          "FHIR is generally better for new implementations in 2025 due to modern technology support and regulatory requirements, while HL7 remains essential for existing internal hospital systems." Used to weight Q2 (systems) and Q5 (infrastructure). rhapsody.health →
        • US — ONC §170.315(g)(10) Final Rule (enforced 2023)
          Requires certified EHRs to support FHIR R4 APIs for standardised patient data access. ONC HTI-1 Rule (2025) extends to USCDI v3 and SMART on FHIR 2.0. ecfr.gov →
        • US — CMS Interoperability and Patient Access Final Rule (CMS-9115)
          Mandates FHIR APIs for payers covering Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, and ACA plans. cms.gov →
        • US — CMS Prior Authorization Rule (CMS-0057-F, effective January 2026)
          Mandates FHIR-based APIs for prior authorization processes between payers and providers, using Da Vinci Implementation Guides. cms.gov →
        • Canada — Canada Health Infoway CA Core+
          Canada's national core set of HL7 FHIR profiles, directional but not yet legislatively mandated federally. Pan-Canadian standards roadmap completion expected 2027. infoway-inforoute.ca →
        • UK — NHS England FHIR UK Core Programme
          NHS has established FHIR as the core standard for health data exchange; over 75% of healthcare institutions implementing FHIR-based mechanisms. digital.nhs.uk →
        • Australia — ADHA National Healthcare Interoperability Plan 2023–2028
          Australian Digital Health Agency tendered FHIR interoperability build for My Health Record (September 2024). Sparked FHIR Accelerator driving national implementation. digitalhealth.gov.au →
        • UAE — DHA Standard DHA/HISHD/ST-02 (Issue 2, effective July 2025)
          Dubai Health Authority mandates both HL7 v2.5 and FHIR-based APIs for NABIDH integration. NABIDH compliance is mandatory for all DHA-licensed facilities. dha.gov.ae →
        • UAE — Malaffi (DoH Abu Dhabi) and Riayati (MOHAP Federal)
          Both platforms use HL7 messaging as primary integration protocol with FHIR APIs for newer application connectivity. sbs-me.com →
        • India — Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission (ABDM), NHA
          India's national digital health mission is built on FHIR. Health Data Management Policy and ABDM specifications use FHIR R4 as the base standard. abdm.gov.in →
        • World Scientific News — Critical Review of Health Data Interoperability Standards (2024) Peer reviewed
          Peer-reviewed analysis confirming FHIR's extensibility and developer-ecosystem advantages, while noting continued HL7 v2 necessity for operational messaging. worldscientificnews.com →
