Plain English — what this resource is saying
This is the patient demographic record for Ahmed Al-Rashid, a 52-year-old male born on 15 March 1973, based in Dubai, UAE. He is reachable on +971-50-123-4567 and is registered under Medical Record Number MRN-78432. His care is coordinated by Dr. Sarah Chen.

The Patient resource does not contain clinical information — no diagnoses, no medications, no results. It is the anchor. Every Observation, Condition, MedicationRequest, and Encounter for Ahmed will contain a reference pointing back to this exact record. Change his demographics here once and all connected resources update.
Encounter resource — What visit did this happen in? The Encounter resource records the outpatient appointment on 1 June 2025. All clinical data from this visit — the blood test, the diagnosis, the prescription — links back to this encounter ID.

Plain English — what this resource is saying
Ahmed attended a finished outpatient consultation at Al Wasl Clinic on 1 June 2025 from 09:00 to 09:45. The appointment was with Dr. Sarah Chen for a Type 2 diabetes mellitus review.

The Encounter resource is the container for this visit. Every result, diagnosis, and order created during those 45 minutes will reference enc-001. This is how a clinician or system can later ask: "What was ordered, diagnosed, and prescribed during this specific visit?" — and get a coherent, linked answer. Without the Encounter, clinical data from different visits would be mixed together with no way to separate them.
Observation resource — What was measured? Ahmed's fasting blood glucose was 7.4 mmol/L — flagged as HIGH, above the normal fasting range. This result is captured as a FHIR Observation, coded so any connected system can act on it automatically.

Plain English — what this resource is saying
On 1 June 2025 at 07:30, Ahmed's fasting blood glucose was measured at 7.4 mmol/L. This is flagged as HIGH — above the normal fasting range of 3.9–6.1 mmol/L. The result was released by Dr. Sarah Chen at 09:15 and is in final status.

The test is coded using LOINC code 1558-6 — an international standard that precisely identifies "fasting glucose in serum or plasma." Any clinical decision support engine, lab system, or remote EHR receiving this resource knows exactly what was measured without any free-text interpretation. Note: Observations are not just for labs — the same structure captures vital signs, social history, survey scores, and clinical assessments.
Condition resource — What is Ahmed's diagnosis? The elevated glucose confirms Type 2 diabetes mellitus — coded with both SNOMED CT and ICD-10 for international interoperability. This resource lives on his active problem list.

Plain English — what this resource is saying
Ahmed has a confirmed, active diagnosis of Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, first identified on 15 May 2025 and formally recorded on 1 June 2025 by Dr. Sarah Chen. It sits on his problem list as an ongoing condition, not just an encounter-specific diagnosis.

The condition is coded with SNOMED CT 44054006 (the global clinical terminology) and ICD-10 E11 (the billing and reporting code). Sending both in one resource means a hospital in Dubai, a GP in London, and a payer system in Toronto can all receive and correctly interpret the same diagnosis without any manual transcription. The verificationStatus: confirmed is critical — it distinguishes this from a provisional or suspected diagnosis, which carry different clinical and administrative implications.
MedicationRequest resource — What was prescribed? Dr. Chen prescribes Metformin 500mg twice daily for 90 days. This is the prescription — structured so any pharmacy system can read, verify, and dispense it without re-entering a single field.

The pattern beneath every resource
Anatomy of a FHIR resource
After reading those five resources, a pattern emerges. Every FHIR resource — regardless of type — answers the same four fundamental questions. Find these fields first and you can read any resource.
Field to look for Question it answers What to expect — and why it matters
resourceType
What kind of data is this?
Always the first field. Tells any system exactly what to do with this payload before reading anything else.
e.g. "Patient", "Observation", "MedicationRequest", "Condition"
subject or patient
Whose data is this?
A reference pointing to the Patient resource. Not the patient's data — just a pointer. Demographics live in one place only.
e.g. "subject": { "reference": "Patient/pat-001", "display": "Ahmed Al-Rashid" }
code or value[x]
What clinical thing happened?
The clinical meaning — a diagnosis, a drug, a lab result. Uses standard terminologies: LOINC for observations, SNOMED CT for clinical concepts, RxNorm for medications, ICD-10 for diagnoses.
e.g. LOINC 1558-6 = fasting glucose  |  SNOMED 44054006 = Type 2 diabetes
status
Is this current, completed, or cancelled?
Every resource has a status lifecycle. Downstream systems use this to decide whether to act on the resource or ignore it. Never assume a resource is actionable without checking status.
Observation: registered → preliminary → final  |  MedicationRequest: active → completed → cancelled
reference
What else does this connect to?
FHIR avoids repeating data. Instead of copying a patient's name into every resource, it uses references. Follow the references and the full clinical picture emerges across linked resources.
e.g. "encounter": { "reference": "Encounter/enc-001" } — links to the visit this happened in
Beyond the four fundamentals — timing fields (effectiveDateTime, authoredOn), who was involved (requester, performer), and intent (intent, verificationStatus) add clinical precision once you are comfortable with the foundation.
